GEOGRAPHY: England / Focus on London Tenure and Housing Demographics
DOCUMENT_TYPE: Housing Stock and Energy Performance Profiles

KEY FINDINGS FOR LONDON HOUSING:

Property types in London:
- 58 percent of London homes are flats or maisonettes
- 24 percent are terraced houses
- 11 percent are semi-detached houses
- 7 percent are detached houses

Tenure in London:
- 27 percent owner occupied
- 29 percent social rented
- 44 percent private rented

This is significantly different from England as a whole where 63 percent are owner occupied.

Energy efficiency in London:
- Average EPC rating in London: D (57 SAP points)
- Only 32 percent of London homes have an EPC rating of C or above
- Private rented sector has the lowest average EPC ratings

Age of housing stock in London:
- 22 percent of London homes were built before 1919
- 31 percent were built between 1919 and 1964
- Older homes are significantly less energy efficient

Insulation in London:
- 58 percent of London homes have loft insulation
- 45 percent have cavity wall insulation
- Only 4 percent have solid wall insulation despite 38 percent of homes having solid walls

Fuel poverty in London:
- 12 percent of London households are in fuel poverty
- Private renters in larger properties face the highest risk
- Households with low income and poor energy efficiency are most affected

Available schemes for London households:
- Warm Home Discount — £150 credit for eligible households
- ECO4 — free insulation and heating improvements for low income households
- Great British Insulation Scheme — free insulation for eligible households
- Mayor of London Warmer Homes — additional London specific support
- Thames Water WaterSure — capped water bills for eligible customers

[Section 1: Tenure and Energy Performance (EPC) Ratings]
The English Housing Survey (EHS) tracks the energy efficiency of the housing stock across different tenure types (owner-occupied, private rented, and social rented). 

Key findings reveal a stark contrast in EPC bands:
- Private Rented Sector (PRS): 
Typically has the lowest Energy Performance Certificate (EPC) profiles of all tenures. Over 50% of privately rented properties fall into EPC Band D or lower. Renters in this sector face "split incentives," where landlords are responsible for insulation and upgrade costs but do not pay the monthly energy bills, leading to under-insulated homes.
- Social Housing / Local Authority:
This sector has the highest average EPC bands (frequently Band C) due to systematic, government-backed local council retrofit schemes and social housing decarbonisation funds.
- Owner-Occupied Homes:
Represents the largest block of housing stock. While homeowners are highly likely to invest in double-glazing (currently an 87% penetration rate), they frequently neglect loft, floor, or wall insulation due to high upfront capital costs and lack of immediate incentives.

[Section 2: Property Age and Solid Walls]
- Homes built before 1919 are the most difficult to heat. Approximately 85% of these older homes have solid brick walls rather than cavity walls. 
- Retrofitting solid walls with internal or external insulation is highly effective but disruptive, whereas homes built post-1944 are much more likely to have cavity walls that are easier and cheaper to insulate.
